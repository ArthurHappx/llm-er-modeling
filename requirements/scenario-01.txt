ER Modeling Scenario
Integrated Hospital Management System for Multiple Facilities
Business Context
You have been hired to model the database for a hospital network that operates 15 facilities across 3 Brazilian states. The system must control, record and monitor everyone who enters and leaves the hospitals, including staff, visitors and patients, ensuring traceability, security and compliance with the institution’s internal rules.
Functional Requirements
1. Hospital
Each hospital has departments responsible for specific activities. Not every facility in the network needs to offer all services: each hospital declares which departments, human resources and capacities it has (available teams and departments).
Departments present in the network:
* Emergency
* ICU (Intensive Care Unit)
* Surgical Center / Operating Theaters
* Adult Ward
* Pediatric Ward
* Maternity
* Radiology / Imaging (X-ray, CT, MRI)
* Clinical Laboratory
* Outpatient Clinic
* Hospital Pharmacy
* Social Services
* Triage
* Reception

1.1. Departments
   * Each department has an assigned coordinator.
   * There must be a leader/responsible person for the department for every shift (i.e., there is always someone responsible for the department at any time).

2. Personnel Management
The hospital receives individuals from multiple categories; some are employees, others are not. Categories include: visitor, patient, physician, nurse, resident physician, researcher and administrative staff.
A single person may assume more than one role (for example: a physician may be admitted as a patient outside their working hours).
      * Physicians may work in multiple facilities under different contract types (e.g., CLT, PJ, on-call/shift, daily rate). (CLT = employment under Brazilian labor law; PJ = contracted as legal entity; “plantonista” = shift/on-call; “diarista” = day-rate worker.)
      * Residents are licensed physicians in training who also see patients under supervision; it is important to record the supervising physician for each resident.
      * Employees may have dependents who are also patients.
      * The hospital records the following for every person who accesses it: full name, addresses, contact phone numbers, CPF, RG and date of birth. (CPF and RG are Brazilian national identifiers; CPF = taxpayer ID; RG = identity card.)
2.1. Patients
      * Each patient receives an identification wristband.
      * The system must record patient movements between departments, including hospital admission, transfers between departments and discharge.
      * A complete history of the departments each patient has visited must be maintained.
      * The system must indicate the patient’s current status: awaiting care, in care, admitted (inpatient), under observation, in surgery, or discharged.
2.2. Visitors
      * Not every visitor is accompanying a patient.
      * The type of visitor must be recorded (e.g., technical visit, regulatory inspection, etc.).
      * Visitor classification is essential to determine access restrictions (important information for the security team).
      * Each visitor receives an identification card at entry.
      * The system must record the visitor’s exit (mandatory).
      * Access outside visiting hours must be prevented unless special authorization is granted by the hospital.
2.3. Employees
      * Employees must be clearly identified.
      * A nurse cannot be recorded as a physician (roles are distinct).
      * Nevertheless, any employee may be treated by the hospital, be present as a visitor, or accompany a patient.
      * Healthcare employees must have their professional credentials recorded (e.g., CRM for physicians and COREN for nurses).
2.4. Addresses
      * A person may have more than one registered address.
      * Employees update their addresses periodically.
      * When possible, patients and visitors also have updated addresses.
      * It is important to store the timestamp of the last address update.
      * If the last update was more than six months ago, the system should alert and request an update of the registration.
2.5. Identification Cards
      * Identification cards have an issue date and an expiry date, recorded in the system for security.
      * Cards have a type that designates access level (visually distinguished by different colors).
      * When a card is in use it is marked active.
      * Because patients and visitors do not have personalized cards, a card’s identifier number may be temporarily linked to the person’s record while in use.
      * Only personalized cards (employees) include a photo.
3. Employee Schedules
The hospital facilities require an integrated time & attendance system covering all 15 facilities to avoid double-booking staff at two locations simultaneously or creating impractical schedules.
      * Staff follow different work patterns and routines.
      * Administration must precisely track the hours worked by each employee.
      * Different hospitals in the network may have different scheduling contracts with the same employee.
      * It is also necessary to record events that remove an employee from duties, such as days off, medical leave, vacation or training.

3.1. Time Clock Records
         * The system must record each employee’s clock-in and clock-out times.
         * As mentioned, every employee has an identification card, which facilitates time recording.
         * Time records should generate alerts if an employee exceeds scheduled hours or misses a clock record (useful for HR).
3.2. Work Shifts
         * Some employees have more flexible shifts than others.
         * Administrative staff generally have fixed weekdays and start/end times.
         * Nurses may follow fixed schedules or work in rotating shifts.
         * Physicians may also have varying schedules, and may come only to perform specific duties (consultations, monitoring, surgeries) and leave when those tasks are completed.
         * The employment contract governs how working hours are administered by the contracting institution.
         * The important point is accurate recording of clock-in and clock-out times for HR verification of compliance.
3.3. Availability Exceptions
         * Reasons that remove a professional from scheduled activities must be well documented.
         * Each exception should include start and end dates, type (day off, medical leave, vacation, training) and an optional textual note.
5. Reports and Queries
         * Query access history by person, date, department and type (employee, visitor, patient).
         * Generate department occupancy reports.
         * Generate reports on compliance with scheduled hours and total hours worked for a given period (month, week, day).
         * The system must alert whenever a department reaches 60%, 80% and 100% of its operational capacity.
